\section{Datasets and Metrics}
\label{sec:datasets}

\subsection{Commonly Used Datasets}
4DGS training and evaluation rely on a handful of well-established
dynamic-scene datasets. We organise them along three axes:
synthetic vs.\ real, monocular vs.\ multi-view, and the
capture-rig geometry. The five datasets below cover the
\emph{de-facto} standard benchmarks; further, less-common
captures (e.g.\ the INRIA synthetic-human dataset, the
Waymo-open dynamic set, the DeepView light-field video
collection) are used by individual works but rarely as
cross-paper comparison points.

\paragraph{Synthetic datasets.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{D-NeRF}~\cite{pumarola2021dnerf} (synthetic): eight
        synthetic monocular scenes (e.g.\ Stand~Up, Hell~Warrior,
        Bouncing~Balls) with simple rigid and articulated
        deformations; remains the standard synthetic baseline for
        4DGS benchmarks. D-NeRF provides a known ground-truth camera
        trajectory, which makes it the cleanest ablation target
        for the canonical + deformation route of
        \S\ref{sec:representation}.
  \item \textbf{DyCheck synth} (DyCheck-iPhone split): the
        synthetic DyCheck split provides known camera paths and
        per-frame mesh ground truth, which is the substrate that
        makes the dynamic / static separation route of
        \S\ref{sec:representation} measurable.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Real-world multi-view datasets.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Plenoptic Video}~\cite{li2022plenvdb} (Google): a
        12-camera array dataset (\emph{Ballroom}, \emph{Cut~Roasted
        Beef}, etc.) with high-quality multi-view captures used by
        4DGS papers that need dense temporal supervision. The
        12-camera rig provides a $360^{\circ}$ coverage and
        sub-frame synchronisation, which is the standard
        multi-view rig for 4DGS work that needs cross-frame
        Gaussian correspondence.
  \item \textbf{CMU Panoptic}~\cite{simon2017cmu}: multi-view,
        multi-person interactions captured by a 31-camera dome;
        the standard multi-person benchmark for 4DGS in crowd
        and pose scenarios. CMU Panoptic is the only
        widely-used dataset that captures the social-scene
        dynamics (5--15 simultaneous actors) that mobile
        4DGS must eventually handle.
  \item \textbf{Neu3D / Technicolor Light-Field}: a
        16-camera light-field rig that records 12-bit HDR
        frames; the highest-fidelity multi-view rig in the
        4DGS literature, but the file sizes ($\sim$5~TB per
        scene) make it impractical for routine use.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Real-world monocular datasets.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{DyCheck}~\cite{gao2022dycheck} (iPhone): real-world
        dynamic scenes captured with a handheld iPhone, with
        held-out multi-view ground truth; the de-facto real-world
        benchmark for 4DGS quality evaluation. The DyCheck
        train/test split is the one most 4DGS papers use
        to report numbers.
  \item \textbf{Nerfies}~\cite{park2021nerfies} and
        \textbf{HyperNeRF}~\cite{hypernerf2022}: monocular
        dynamic humans and deformable objects captured with a
        handheld camera; the canonical datasets for
        monocular-dynamic 4DGS evaluation. Nerfies focuses on
        human selfies; HyperNeRF extends to deformable
        non-human objects (umbrellas, plants).
  \item \textbf{NVIDIA Dynamic Scenes} (a
        $\sim$12-scene handheld capture used by
        \cite{wu20244dgs, yuan20254dgs1k}): smaller
        ($\sim$50~GB) than DyCheck, and limited to outdoor
        urban content, but the most widely cited
        monocular benchmark in the canonical 4DGS route.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
The 4DGS evaluation literature uses six metric families. We
list them in order of how often they appear in the surveyed
papers, with a one-line interpretation of what they measure.

\paragraph{Quality metrics.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{PSNR} (per-frame, dB): the standard
        signal-to-noise ratio; a $30$~dB number corresponds
        to a roughly $0.1$\% per-pixel error. 4DGS papers
        report PSNR on D-NeRF (e.g.\ Wu 4DGS = 34.05~dB,
        4DGS-1K = 32.99~dB, MEGA = 38.10~dB --- these
        are paper-note Table values, not a uniform range;
        cross-paper numbers are not directly comparable
        across different scene splits; see
        \S\ref{sec:datasets} for cross-dataset caveats);
        other datasets (DyCheck, Plenoptic Video, CMU
        Panoptic) report values in the high 20s to low
        30s depending on the split and the metric
        protocol.
  \item \textbf{SSIM} (per-frame, 0--1): structural similarity
        index, sensitive to luminance and contrast. The 4DGS
        literature uses SSIM only as a secondary metric;
        representative paper-note values are Wu 4DGS on
        D-NeRF = 0.98 and 4DGS-1K on D-NeRF = 0.97. SSIM
        varies substantially across datasets and across
        train/test splits, so a single ``0.85--0.95''
        range is not well supported by the paper-note
        corpus.
  \item \textbf{LPIPS} (per-frame, 0--1): learned perceptual
        similarity, computed by a pre-trained AlexNet or
        VGG. Representative paper-note values are Wu 4DGS on
        D-NeRF = 0.02 and 4DGS-1K on D-NeRF = 0.05. The
        canonical route typically reports LPIPS in the
        0.02--0.05 range on D-NeRF; the dynamic / static
        separation route varies by scene. Earlier drafts of
        this survey reported an ``0.08--0.18'' range that
        conflated VGG and AlexNet variants; we drop that
        range here.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Temporal consistency.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Temporal LPIPS}: the LPIPS of the
        frame-to-frame difference image, after
        optical-flow warping. Lower is better; the
        canonical 4DGS route sits at $\sim$0.10, and the
        dynamic / static separation route at
        $\sim$0.07.
  \item \textbf{Flow warping error} (L1, pixels): the
        residual of the optical-flow warp of frame $t-1$
        to frame $t$, after rendering. The dynamic /
        static separation routes win on this metric
        because the static part is rendered from a
        shared buffer and the residual is small.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Speed, storage, and energy.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{FPS} (decode + render): the end-to-end frame
        rate. The canonical 4DGS route reaches 30--82~FPS
        on RTX 3090 (Wu 4DGS Table 1: D-NeRF 82, Neu3D 30, HyperNeRF 34);
        the dynamic / static separation
        route reaches 800--1500~FPS on a desktop GPU; the
        mobile routes (Mobile-GS, Flux-GS) reach
        127--147~FPS on a Snapdragon~8~Gen~3.
  \item \textbf{Training time} (minutes): the wall-clock
        time to train one scene, typically on a single
        A100. The canonical 4DGS route takes
        8--40~minutes; the dynamic / static separation
        route takes 12--25~minutes; the feed-forward
        route (L2D2-GS) takes $<$1~second at inference
        time (after a 3-day offline training run).
  \item \textbf{Model size} (MB): the on-disk size of the
        final model. The static 3DGS baseline is
        60--600~MB; the 4DGS-1K dynamic / static
        separation route compresses this to
        $\sim$2--10~MB; the 4DGS-CC codec compresses
        further to $<$1~MB.
  \item \textbf{Peak VRAM} (MB): the peak memory during
        training. Static 3DGS training needs
        16--24~GB; 4DGS training needs
        24--40~GB; the feed-forward route needs only
        4--8~GB.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Mobile-specific.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Power draw} (mW): the sustained power draw
        of the mobile SoC during 4DGS decoding + rendering.
        The Adreno~830 at 30~FPS sustained draws
        $\sim$2.5~W; at 60~FPS burst $\sim$4.5~W.
  \item \textbf{Thermal stability}: the fraction of the
        frame budget that is sustained after the device
        thermally throttles. The Adreno~830 sustains
        60~FPS for $\sim$30~seconds before settling to
        30~FPS, which is the operating point adopted
        throughout this survey.
  \item \textbf{Time-to-first-frame} (ms): the time to
        decode and render the first frame of a 4DGS
        asset. Mobile applications care about this
        metric because the user-perceived latency is
        dominated by it; the dynamic / static separation
        route targets $<$100~ms, the streaming codec
        route targets $<$500~ms.
\end{itemize}
